# The Hormuz Chokehold: How the World's Most Critical Oil Lane Became a Powder Keg

*April 7, 2026*

In late February, US and Israeli airstrikes killed Iranian Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei. Within days, Iranian forces closed the Strait of Hormuz — the narrow waterway through which roughly 20 million barrels of oil flow every single day. Six weeks later, the world is staring down the largest oil supply disruption in recorded history, a cascade of economic pain that analysts say could be worse than the 1970s energy shocks and the Russia-Ukraine war combined.

## How We Got Here

The crisis did not come from nowhere. It was the culmination of failed nuclear negotiations in Geneva and a prior 12-day air conflict in 2025. When Operation Epic Fury struck Tehran on February 28, 2026, Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) responded the only way it could credibly threaten the global economy: by sealing the strait.

Container shipping giants Maersk and CMA CGM suspended Hormuz operations by March 2. Iran began laying naval mines by March 10. By March 27, the IRGC announced a complete closure to vessels bound for US, Israeli, or allied ports. Saudi Arabia cut output 20%. Iraq slashed production 70% from key fields. Qatar and Kuwait declared force majeure. In just four weeks, approximately 10 million barrels per day — more than 90% of normal strait throughput — was effectively cut from global markets.

The oil price surge was faster than during any previous conflict in modern history: faster than the Gulf War, faster than the Iraq War, faster than the Ukraine invasion. Brent crude crossed $100 on March 8 for the first time in four years. Dubai crude hit a record $166 on March 19. WTI is now sitting above $112 as negotiations drag into April, with no clear resolution in sight.

## The $200 Scenario

Wall Street is no longer treating $200-per-barrel oil as a tail risk — it's becoming a base case if the crisis extends through summer.

Deutsche Bank has modeled a worst-case scenario of $200/bbl. Goldman Sachs projects $120–$150 in a prolonged conflict. JPMorgan estimates $120 if the standoff exceeds three weeks — a threshold already long passed. BlackRock CEO Larry Fink has warned that $150 oil would trigger a "steep and stark" global recession. The Economist estimates that two months at $140 would push major economies into a slump.

IEA chief Fatih Birol has been even starker: "No nation would be immune. This is worse than the twin oil shocks of the 1970s and the Russia-Ukraine war combined."

The scale backs that up. The 1973 Arab oil embargo removed 4–5 million barrels per day from markets. The current disruption threatens 13–20 million. The math is brutal.

## Beyond Oil: A Compound Shock

The Hormuz crisis is not just an energy crisis — it is a food security crisis and a shipping crisis arriving simultaneously.

One-third of the world's fertilizer trade passes through the strait. Gulf producers supply nearly half of global urea and 30% of ammonia. Urea prices have already surged 50% since the war began. Farmers from India to Brazil are facing sharply higher input costs heading into planting season.

Over 150 tankers are anchored in Gulf waters, unable to move. Major shipping lines have rerouted via the Cape of Good Hope, adding 2–3 weeks to voyage times and driving up freight rates. For the first time in modern history, both Hormuz and the Bab el-Mandeb (at the southern Red Sea) face simultaneous chokepoint pressure — a dual blockade scenario shipping analysts had long considered hypothetical.

## Where Things Stand

As of April 7, negotiations between the US, Iran, and regional mediators are circling a potential 45-day ceasefire. Trump has threatened to "destroy every bridge and power plant in Iran" if the strait is not reopened. Polymarket prediction markets show US forces entering Iran at 99.7% probability before April 30 — an extraordinary signal of how traders are pricing the geopolitical risk.

The ceasefire window is narrow. CPI data due April 10 will show just how far inflation has already run; Bloomberg Economics puts US CPI for March at 3.4% year-over-year, up from 2.4% in February, with fuel the primary driver. A $170-per-barrel scenario, analysts warn, would roughly double that inflation shock — creating a stagflationary trap that would complicate every major central bank's rate-setting calculus.

The Hormuz strait is 21 miles wide at its narrowest point. Right now, it is the most consequential 21 miles on the planet.
